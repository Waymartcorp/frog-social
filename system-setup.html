New Xenopus System Setup

This page is a structured checklist for bringing a new Xenopus system online: planning, cycling, stocking, and the first 90 days. It is written from a husbandry-first perspective — the goal is a stable, readable environment before the stocking the research cohort.

1. Planning & design

Decisions to confirm before you buy or install

  • Recirculating rack vs static or dump-and-fill tanks (or a hybrid).
  • Intended capacity: number of adult frogs, breeding intensity, embryo work.
  • Room: temperature band, HVAC stability, light cycle, vibration sources.
  • Water source: municipal, RO + reconstitution, or well water.
  • Drain / waste handling and spill management.

A simple one-page “system intent” document (room, water, capacity, use-case) is worth keeping and attaching to the colony register entry.

2. Hardware & installation

Rack / tank hardware

  • Confirm materials are amphibian-appropriate (no untreated copper or mystery metals).
  • Check all joints, valves, and unions for leak-free operation before adding animals.
  • Verify that flow to each tank is gentle and adjustable.
  • Position airlines, stones, and returns to avoid strong jets on frogs.

Filters & media

  • Assemble mechanical, biological, and (if used) carbon/chemical stages.
  • Rinse new media to remove fines and dust before cycling.
  • Make sure there is easy access for routine cleaning without dismantling the system.

3. Water preparation & cycling

Initial fill

  • Decide on salt formulation / conductivity band for this system.
  • Fill with conditioned source water (e.g., dechlorinated municipal, RO + salts).
  • Bring system to target temperature and pH range before cycling.

Biological cycling

  • Seed biofilters with a trusted inoculum if available, or allow slow fishless cycling.
  • Monitor ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate over days to weeks.
  • Only consider the system “ready” once ammonia and nitrite are consistently zero or near zero under a test load.

Do not rush cycling just to receive animals; it is better to adjust delivery dates than to start with an unstable biofilter.

4. Stocking strategy

Initial animals

  • Start with a modest number of frogs relative to system capacity.
  • Group by size and sex to avoid extreme dominance or overcrowding in any single tank.
  • Log source (vendor or in-house), dates, and tank assignments from day one.

Quarantine approach

  • Consider a dedicated quarantine zone or rack for new arrivals.
  • Establish separate equipment (nets, siphons) for quarantine vs main system.
  • Record any procedures (swabs, sampling) without letting them dominate welfare decisions.

5. First 90 days: monitoring & adjustments

Parameters to track

  • Temperature, pH, and conductivity at least weekly (more often at first).
  • Ammonia/nitrite after any major change or new stocking event.
  • Feeding patterns and waste load (uneaten food, feces buildup).
  • Behavior: hiding, floating, aggression, abnormal movement.

Adjustments

  • Make small, reversible changes rather than multiple big shifts at once.
  • Document what you changed, when, and why in the colony register or notes.
  • Use Frog Social’s case-intake when you see patterns you don’t yet understand.

6. Linking setup to Frog Social

  • Create a Colony / Room entry for this system in the colony register.
  • Attach basic setup notes (water recipe, target ranges, rack type, room ID).
  • When issues arise (mortality, behavior, embryo survival, extracts, oocytes), use Describe a Problem and link back to this system.

Over time, new-system setups and their case histories become a shared library: what worked, what drifted, and how labs resolved problems without overreacting to isolated pathogen findings.
