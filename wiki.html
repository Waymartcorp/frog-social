Solution recipes (working starting points)

Exact formulations vary slightly by lab tradition. These are typical values and starting points, to be refined and confirmed locally.

Barth’s / Modified Barth’s (stock + working)

Show Barth’s stock & working solution

Example 10× Barth’s stock (dissolve in distilled water, adjust pH 7.4–7.8):

  • NaCl — 880 mM
  • KCl — 10 mM
  • MgSO₄·7H₂O — 25 mM
  • Ca(NO₃)₂·4H₂O — 8 mM
  • CaCl₂·6H₂O — 10 mM
  • NaHCO₃ — 250 mM
  • HEPES — 100 mM

Working Barth’s / MBS: dilute 1:10 in distilled water and re-check pH. Many labs add antibiotics or adjust osmolarity; record any deviations you adopt.

0.1× MMR (standard embryo medium)

Show 0.1× MMR composition
  • NaCl — 10 mM
  • KCl — 0.2 mM
  • MgSO₄ — 0.1 mM
  • CaCl₂ — 0.1 mM
  • HEPES — 5 mM (pH ~7.8–7.9 with NaOH)

Prepare as a 10× stock if helpful, then dilute 1:10 for 0.1× working solution. Always document the pH you actually use and whether you add antibiotics or other supplements.

ND96 (oocyte recording buffer)

Show ND96 composition
  • NaCl — 96 mM
  • KCl — 2 mM
  • CaCl₂ — 1.8 mM
  • MgCl₂ — 1 mM
  • HEPES — 5 mM (pH ~7.5)

Variants include added antibiotics and modified divalent concentrations. For troubleshooting, it is critical to record which ND96 variant was used, how often it was changed, and how pH was verified.

System water & environment targets

These are typical working bands, not hard limits. Stability and trend awareness are often more important than a single measurement.

  • Temperature: 18–20 °C preferred; 18–22 °C common.
  • pH: roughly 7.0–8.0, stable within a narrow band over days.
  • Conductivity: commonly ~800–2200 µS depending on system design.
  • Ammonia: target non-detectable; persistent detection is a red flag.
  • Flow: gentle turnover without strong jets; avoid chronic vibration.
  • Lighting: consistent photoperiod; avoid extremes and sudden changes.

When logging a case in Frog Social, record actual values where possible, and make note of drift over the previous 2–4 weeks, not just “today.”

Embryo & tadpole troubleshooting (very high level)

Developmental failures often cluster in “bands” of time. The goal is to pair the when (stage/time) with likely environmental or procedural stressors.

Very early loss (0–12 hours post-fertilization)

  • Fertilization failure or poor egg quality.
  • Large pH swings or incorrect osmolarity of fertilization medium.
  • Temperature shocks immediately post-fertilization.

Gastrulation / neurulation issues

  • Overly alkaline MMR or medium (pH drifting well above target).
  • Temperature fluctuations during cleavage/gastrulation.
  • Mechanical disturbance (vibration, rough handling).

Later tadpole losses

  • Ammonia buildup and poor water change routines.
  • Inadequate aeration / oxygenation as biomass increases.
  • Inadequate food particle size or frequency (underfeeding or fouling).

The case-intake page is the right place to document these patterns in detail; this section is only a scaffold to remind people what to look for.

Oocyte handling & ion channel basics (scaffold)

This section is not a complete protocol, but a checklist of domains that matter when troubleshooting oocyte recordings or expression.

Defolliculation

  • Collagenase lot, concentration, and incubation time.
  • Temperature and mixing intensity during digestion.
  • Gentle rinsing and removal of enzyme when finished.

Storage conditions

  • Buffer: which ND96 variant, additives, and exact pH.
  • Temperature stability in the storage incubator or room.
  • How often solutions are changed (e.g., daily or more often).
  • Routine removal of dead/unhealthy oocytes from the dish.

RNA / cRNA & injection

  • Integrity and concentration of RNA/cRNA; storage and freeze–thaw history.
  • Injection volume, needle calibration, and technique consistency.
  • Timing between injection and recording.

In many “bad recording” cases, these lab-side variables dominate over colony or rack conditions. Frog Social’s case-intake can help capture both sides without defaulting to “blame the frogs.”

Husbandry-first welfare notes

A central premise of the Xenopus Model Welfare effort is that colony health is driven primarily by environment, behavior, and long-term husbandry, with pathogens acting mainly as opportunists.

Pseudocapillaroides xenopi context

P. xenopi is frequently present in Xenopus colonies at low levels without obvious disease. Widespread lesions, poor condition, or systemic signs should trigger investigation into husbandry stressors (aging animals, water quality, density, diet, flow, vibration), rather than an immediate move toward elimination of every detectable organism.

Pathogen and PCR data can be logged in cases, but they should be interpreted within this broader environmental context rather than as sole health metrics.
